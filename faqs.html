Your tax code tells your employer how much Income Tax to take from your pay. It usually has numbers and letters.

Key Parts:
Numbers: Show your tax-free allowance. For example, if your code is 1257L, you can earn ¬£12,570 before paying tax.

Letters: Explain your tax situation. Common ones include:
L:Standard tax-free amount.
M: You've received the Marriage Allowance from your partner.
N: You've given the Marriage Allowance to your partner.
BR: All your income is taxed at the basic rate (often if you have a second job).
Example:
If your code is 1257L, you can earn ¬£12,570 tax-free.
Your tax code can change, so it's important to check your payslips and contact HMRC if something looks wrong.

If you're overpaid, your employer will notify you about the mistake and explain how they'll recover the excess amount. They might deduct it from your future wages or set up a repayment plan. It's important to check your payslips and contact your payroll department if you notice any discrepancies.

The ACAS guidelines state that an employer should not deduct money from their employee's wages without letting them know.

It is worth noting that within the ACAS guidelines the word 'should' is used to indicate what Acas considers to be good employment practice, rather than legal requirements. The word 'must' is used to indicate where something is a legal requirement.
