Gross Pay refers to the total income earned before any deductions for Income Tax and National Insurance. It includes:

Basic Salary/Income: Your regular earnings.

Additional Earnings: This can include bonuses, overtime or holiday pay for casual hours worked.

This figure represents your total earnings before any taxes or contributions are deducted. It's important to understand Gross Pay as it forms the basis for calculating your net pay (take-home pay) after all deductions.

Personal Allowance: All UK resident individuals and certain non-resident individuals earning under £100,000 per year are entitled to a personal allowance of £12,570. This amount of income can be earned tax-free. Any income over this allowance is subject to tax.

Your tax code tells your employer how much Income Tax to take from your pay. It usually has numbers and letters.

Numbers: Show your tax-free allowance. For example, if your code is 1257L, you can earn £12,570 before paying tax.

Letters: Explain your tax situation. Common ones include:

L: Standard tax-free amount.
M: You've received the Marriage Allowance from your partner.
N: You've given the Marriage Allowance to your partner.
BR: All your income is taxed at the basic rate (often if you have a second job).

Example of Tax Codes:

880L: Indicates that the employee has some reason for a restricted personal allowance of £8,880. Next is the L, this denotes that the person is under 65 years young and they earn under £100,000 per year.

Your tax code can change, so it's important to check your payslips and contact HMRC if something looks wrong.

Check Your Payslip: Your tax code will be displayed on your payslip. Ensure it matches the tax code provided by HMRC.

Review your Personal Tax Account: Log in to your Personal Tax Account on the HMRC website to view your tax code and details about how it's calculated.

Check for Changes: If you’ve had changes in your job, salary, or personal allowances (like Marriage Allowance), your tax code may have been updated.

Contact HMRC: If you think your tax code is incorrect, contact HMRC. They can confirm your correct tax code and issue a new one if necessary.

If you're overpaid, your employer will notify you about the mistake and explain how they'll recover the excess amount. They might deduct it from your future wages or set up a repayment plan. It's important to check your payslips and contact your payroll department if you notice any discrepancies.

The ACAS guidelines state that an employer should not deduct money from their employee's wages without letting them know.

It is worth noting that within the ACAS guidelines the word 'should' is used to indicate what Acas considers to be good employment practice, rather than legal requirements. The word 'must' is used to indicate where something is a legal requirement.

National Insurance (NI): National Insurance (NI) is a system of contributions paid by workers and employers in the UK to fund state benefits like the State Pension, unemployment benefits, and the NHS. Both employees and employers make National Insurance contributions (NICs), which are deducted from wages.

Purpose:

State Pension: Secures your pension upon retirement.
Welfare Benefits: Supports the unemployed, disabled, or those on maternity leave.
NHS Funding: Contributes to the National Health Service.

Types of NICs:

Class 1: Paid by employees and employers on earnings.
Class 2: Paid by self-employed individuals.
Class 3: Voluntary contributions to fill gaps in the NI record.
Class 4: Additional contributions paid by self-employed individuals on profits.

Deduction Process:

Earnings Thresholds: NICs are based on earnings; different rates apply above and below certain thresholds.
PAYE System: Employers use the Pay As You Earn (PAYE) system to deduct NICs from wages before they are paid to you.
Payslips: Your payslip details the NICs deducted along with other deductions like Income Tax.
